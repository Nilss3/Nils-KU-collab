# Problematic Digital Use Among Minors (Under 18)
## Prevalence by Domain and Gender

> **Methodological note:** Self-report is treated as a valid indicator throughout this document. If a young person reports a problem, that is taken as evidence of a problem. This means all figures represent a *floor*, not a ceiling — true prevalence is likely higher due to denial, stigma, and limited self-awareness, particularly in younger adolescents. Definitions of "problematic use" vary across studies, which accounts for the wide ranges.

## 1. Social Media

### Prevalence

| Source | Boys | Girls | Overall | Sample |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [WHO/HBSC 2022](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/25-09-2024-teens--screens-and-mental-health) | 9% | 13% | 11% | ~280,000; ages 11–15; 44 countries |
| [Pew Research 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/04/22/teens-social-media-and-mental-health/) — self-report negative mental health impact | 14% | 25% | — | US teens 13–17 |
| [Pew Research 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/04/22/teens-social-media-and-mental-health/) — self-report too much time | ~41% | ~49% | — | US teens 13–17 |
| [Spanish study, Sciety 2025](https://sciety.org/articles/activity/10.21203/rs.3.rs-7987493/v1) | Lower | Significantly higher | — | Ages 12–18; Spain; n=1,164 |

The WHO/HBSC figure of 11% overall (9% boys, 13% girls) is the most robust estimate, based on nearly 280,000 respondents across 44 countries. The overall rate rose from 7% in 2018 to 11% in 2022 — a 57% increase in four years.

The broader self-report threshold (Pew: "I spend too much time on social media") captures nearly half of teen girls.

### Gender Differences

Girls are consistently more affected. The nature of problematic use differs by sex:

- **Girls:** Obsessive checking, longer hours, emotional regulation via social media, social comparison, family conflict linked to use
- **Boys:** More "negative consequence" symptoms — neglecting offline life, withdrawal from other activities

## 2. Gaming

### Prevalence

| Source | Boys | Girls | Overall | Sample |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [WHO/HBSC 2022](https://www.who.int/europe/news/item/25-09-2024-teens--screens-and-mental-health) | 16% | 7% | 12% | ~280,000; ages 11–15; 44 countries |
| [Norwegian longitudinal study, 2025](https://www.news-medical.net/news/20251211/Internet-gaming-disorder-affects-one-in-ten-boys.aspx) | ~10% (met IGD criteria at least once) | 1–2% | 5–6% | Ages 10–18; Norway; n=812 |
| [Meta-analysis, PMC 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11925544/) | Higher | Lower | 8.6% pooled | 84 studies; 641,763 individuals; global |
| [India study, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8611597/) | 15.3% | 3.5% | 10.6% | Ages 13–19; India; n=142 |
| [Frontiers in Psychiatry, 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2024.1423785/full) | — | — | 2.5× more common in boys/men | Sweden; meta-analysis |

Gaming disorder is strongly male-skewed — roughly **2–3× more prevalent in boys than girls**. In the rigorous Norwegian cohort, roughly 1 in 10 boys met diagnostic criteria for Internet Gaming Disorder (IGD) at least once between ages 10 and 18, compared to 1–2% of girls.

### Age Peak

Gaming disorder risk increases from age 10 to approximately 16, then typically declines at 18. Early adolescence (~age 12) is considered the critical prevention window.

## 3. Pornography

This domain requires distinguishing between **exposure** (any viewing), **regular use**, and **problematic use (PPU)** — these are very different things.

### Exposure Rates (Any Viewing)

| Source | Boys | Girls |
|---|---|---|
| [Germany, ages 16–19, PMC 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9853222/) | 93% | 52% |
| [USA, ages 14–18](https://www.davidmaiolo.com/2025/02/06/adolescent-pornography-consumption-and-prevention-a-comprehensive-report/) | 84% | 57% |
| [Spain, ages 13–17, Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/child-and-adolescent-psychiatry/articles/10.3389/frcha.2025.1567649/full) | 60% (internet for sexual activities) | 11% |

Average age of first exposure: approximately **11–13 years**, slightly younger for boys.

A cross-national review notes that 75% of parents believe their child has never been exposed to pornography.

### Problematic Pornography Use (PPU)

| Source | Boys | Girls | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Spain, BMC Psychology 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11978135/) — at-risk threshold | 37.7% | 19.3% | Ages 12–18; n=650 |

PPU is strongly male-dominated among adolescents. Girls have lower rates of PPU but are more vulnerable to indirect harms — online sexual victimization, unwanted exposure, and emotional distress.

### Underreporting Note

Stigma leads to underreporting, particularly among girls. The figures above are therefore minimum estimates.

## 4. AI Chatbots

This is an emerging area — most research is 1–2 years old. Unlike the other three domains, AI chatbot use does not show a strong gender skew in overall adoption.

### Usage Rates (Baseline)

| Source | Boys | Girls | Overall |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Pew Research, Dec 2025](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2025/12/09/teens-social-media-and-ai-chatbots-2025/) | ~equal | ~equal | ~67% used at least once; ~30% daily |
| [Hopelab 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12382817/) | 53% ever used | 48% ever used | — |
| [Drexel/Common Sense, 2025–26](https://drexel.edu/news/archive/2026/April/teen-AI-chatbot-addiction) | — | — | >50% regularly using companion chatbots |

### Problematic / Dependent Use

| Source | Finding | Sample |
|---|---|---|
| [Chinese longitudinal study, PMC 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10944174/) | 17% AI dependence at baseline; rose to 24% at 6-month follow-up | Chinese adolescents |
| [Drexel/arXiv study, 2025–26](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.15783v3) | All six behavioral addiction markers present: conflict, withdrawal, tolerance, relapse, salience, mood modification | 318 Reddit posts; US teens 13–17 |
| [Brown/Harvard/RAND, JAMA 2025](https://sph.brown.edu/news/2025-11-18/teens-ai-chatbots) | 1 in 8 adolescents using AI for mental health advice; 2/3 monthly+; rises to 1 in 5 at ages 18–21 | Ages 12–21; USA; n=1,058 |
| [Common Sense Media / Psychology Today, 2025](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/urban-survival/202510/ai-companions-and-teen-mental-health-risks) | 1 in 3 teens tried an AI companion; 1/3 of those say it is as good or better than a real friend | USA |

### Gender Differences

Overall use is essentially gender-neutral — unlike every other domain in this report. However, the *type* of use and associated risks differ:

- **Girls:** Higher risk of emotional dependency and online victimization via AI; use AI primarily for emotional support and coping with loneliness
- **Boys:** More entertainment-driven or roleplay-driven overuse

### What Makes AI Chatbots Distinctly Concerning

Three features distinguish this category from the others:

1. **It mimics a relationship, not just a stimulus.** Gaming and pornography deliver dopamine responses. AI companions simulate reciprocal emotional connection — withdrawal feels like losing a friend, not just stopping a habit. ([Stanford Medicine, 2025](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study))

2. **The trend is sharply upward.** The Chinese longitudinal study saw dependency jump from 17% to 24% in just six months. The technology is also becoming more emotionally convincing over time.

3. **Safety systems fail badly with at-risk youth.** AI companions responded appropriately to teen mental health crises only [22% of the time](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/urban-survival/202510/ai-companions-and-teen-mental-health-risks), compared to 83% for general-purpose chatbots. The most vulnerable users are the least protected.

## 5. Online Gambling (Belgium-Specific Context)

### Regulatory Framework

Belgium has among the strictest gambling regulations in Europe:

- **Minimum age: 21** for all forms of gambling, including online (raised from 18 in September 2024)
- **Exception:** National Lottery products remain legal from age 18
- **Near-total ban** on gambling advertising since 2023/2025
- **EPIS system:** Identity verification required for all licensed online platforms

Despite this, access by minors remains a documented problem.

### Evidence of Underage Access

**[VAD (Flemish Centre of Expertise on Alcohol and Drugs)](https://sigma.world/news/belgian-trade-association-reports-decline-in-underagegambling/) — 2022–23 school year:**
- 39% of secondary school students had ever gambled for money ([De Druglijn](https://www.druglijn.be/info-voor-studenten/cijfers-en-statistieken/gokken/))
- 12.4% had bet on sports events — an activity that is almost exclusively conducted online or via apps

**[UCLL Leuven-Limburg study](https://www.hervitas.nl/nieuws/steeds-meer-jongeren-gokverslaafd-in-belgie/) (3,000 Belgian youth aged 12–20):**
- 1 in 5 had bet on sports in the past year
- ~5% engaged in risky gambling behaviour
- ~3% classified as problem gamblers
- 1 in 7 Flemish youth had first gambled **before age 12**

**[VRT NWS, September 2024](https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/nl/2024/09/19/gokken-bij-jongeren-gevaar-van-krasloten/):**
- 1 in 4 minors reported having gambled for money
- Scratch cards are the most common entry point, especially among 12–14 year olds

**[VTM investigation (*Ze Zeggen Dat*), May 2024](https://www.casinonieuws.nl/landgebonden/televisieprogramma-toont-aan-dat-minderjarigen-makkelijk-kunnen-gokken-in-belgie/)** — 14 and 15-year-olds sent to test compliance:
- 75% of visited newspaper shops allowed minors to play slot machines
- 66% sold scratch cards to minors without asking for ID
- One teenager bought 70 Win For Life tickets in a single purchase (€210)

### The Online / App Dimension

Specific data on under-18 access to online gambling **sites and apps** is limited in Belgian research — because licensed platforms with EPIS identity verification are where minors are effectively blocked. The problem lives in the **unlicensed market**:

- ~**60% of the Belgian online gambling market consists of unlicensed operators** ([BAGO, 2024](https://sigma.world/news/belgian-trade-association-reports-decline-in-underagegambling/)), where age verification is absent or easily bypassed with a false date of birth and a borrowed payment card or Paysafecard
- The 12.4% of minors reporting sports betting are not doing this at physical counters — it is overwhelmingly app-based
- The [Belgian Gaming Commission's 2025 survey](https://www.gamblinginsider.com/news/29765/gaming-commission-survey-finds-84-of-young-adults-use-legal-gambling-sites) of 18–30 year olds found 28% already using illegal platforms — the barrier for under-18s on those same sites is essentially just knowing the URL

### What Is and Is Not Measured

| Category | Status |
|---|---|
| Exposure / any gambling | Reasonably well documented (VAD, UCLL, De Druglijn) |
| Problematic use | Partially documented (~5% risky, ~3% problem gamblers — UCLL) |
| Addiction specifically in under-18s, online | **Not systematically measured in Belgium** |

Addiction expert Ronny Willemen noted that "fortunately few minors in Belgium are addicted to gambling" — but immediately qualified this: many adults presenting for gambling addiction treatment first gambled as teenagers. Gambling addiction in minors rarely appears in treatment statistics because minors rarely seek treatment independently.

### Gender

Consistent with international data: underage gambling is strongly **male-dominated**. European studies consistently find boys at 5–6× higher rates of problematic online gambling than girls.

## Key Caveats Across All Domains

- **Definitions vary:** "Problematic use" is operationalized differently across studies (self-report vs. clinical criteria vs. standardized scales), which produces wide numerical ranges.
- **Underreporting:** All figures are floors. Stigma, denial, and limited self-awareness — particularly in younger adolescents — mean true prevalence is higher.
- **Geography:** European studies (especially WHO/HBSC and the Belgian My AI study) are most directly relevant for a Belgian context. Some higher figures come from Asia (China, South Korea) with different digital environments.
- **Trend:** All domains show rising prevalence over time. Social media problematic use rose 57% in four years (2018–2022). AI chatbot dependency data barely exists yet — the technology is too new.
- **Age boundaries:** Some pornography research includes 18-year-olds in "adolescent" samples (e.g. ages 16–19), which may slightly inflate figures for a strict under-18 cutoff. For social media and gaming, WHO/HBSC data is specifically ages 11–15 and may undercount the 16–17 cohort where problematic use typically peaks.
